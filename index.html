Design analysis of Matt's career timeline

A review of the experience site on the develop branch, with recommendations for making it land with recruiters.

lume / design-intelligence / june 21 2026

Context

Matt built a career timeline site to supplement job applications. It's vibe-coded, hosted on GitHub Pages, and lives on the develop branch of matthesoundman/experience.

The career story is strong: Navy electronics, then Apple RF systems, then Microsoft driver quality. Each step builds on the last. The timeline format is the right choice for this narrative.

He filed 5 issues against himself and asked for formatting help. He said "don't spend much time on it." This review respects that while identifying what actually matters for his goal.

What's working

The career narrative

Three employers, clear skill escalation, and a throughline. This is a better story than most resumes tell. The timeline format lets a viewer absorb it in seconds.

Dark theme with frosted nav

The fixed backdrop-filter: blur(18px) nav bar is clean. The dark tone is appropriate for a technical portfolio. The foundation is solid.

The degree band concept

Showing the engineering degree as a connecting element between the Apple card and the timeline communicates "I did this while working full time" visually. Clever structural choice.

Scroll-reveal animations

The IntersectionObserver-based reveals are subtle and appropriate. Good performance characteristics -- GPU-accelerated opacity and transform only.

Matt's filed issues

Five issues he logged against himself. All valid, all fixable.

#1
Parallel events display incorrectly
Education concurrent with work doesn't render right. Technical Schools (2008--2013, during Navy service) appears sequentially after Microsoft instead of alongside Navy.
#2
Card symbols aren't great
Needs the Navy ET rating symbol and a proper Microsoft icon. Current icons: obscure Unicode, Apple logo (only renders on Apple devices), empty square, triangle.
#3
Technical schools card is too big
Should be subtle like the degree band, not a full-size card. The .compact class is applied in HTML but has no CSS -- intended but never implemented.
#4
Website header needs review
"Does not serve well at the moment." The hero takes 92vh before any timeline content appears.
#5
LinkedIn ID is missing
The placeholder YOUR_PROFILE is still in the LinkedIn href.

AI design fingerprints

The critical finding. The site reads as AI-generated within seconds. For a job application supplement, this undercuts the implicit message: "I care about quality."

detected

Rainbow accent palette. Five competing colors. Design principle: one accent. Multiple accents is the most common AI palette signature.

detected

Neon outer glows. box-shadow: 0 0 38px on timeline dots. Classic AI glow. Subtle inner borders or tinted shadows read as human-designed.

detected

Identical card structure. Every card has the same proportions, layout, icon treatment. Human designers vary by type and importance.

detected

CSS grid overlay. The 36px grid pattern on body::before. Common AI dark-theme cliche. Noise without signal.

detected

Oversized centered hero. 92vh hero with huge H1 and tight letter-spacing. Textbook AI landing page. The hero should earn its space or get out of the way.

detected

Generic Unicode icons. Arbitrary Unicode characters as icons. The Apple logo character only renders on Apple devices -- Windows recruiters see a broken glyph.

Through the recruiter lens

Matt's audience arrives on mobile (LinkedIn, click, phone browser), spends 10--20 seconds, and wants to immediately grasp the career trajectory.

92vh hero pushes content below the fold

The timeline -- the reason someone clicks the link -- is invisible until they scroll past a near-full-screen hero. On mobile, this is especially costly.

Navigation disappears on mobile

Below 850px, both nav links are hidden. The "Path" and "Resume" links vanish on the device recruiters are actually using.

No Open Graph meta tags

When shared on LinkedIn or pasted into an application, the link preview is generic. No title, no description, no image. The meta tags are what the recruiter sees before they click.

Cross-platform icon rendering

The Apple card uses a Unicode character that only works on macOS and iOS. Windows recruiters see a broken glyph. SVGs solve this.

Recommendations

What Matt asked for, and what would make the biggest difference.

tier 1 -- his 5 issues

Formatting fixes, icon swaps, parallel layout for concurrent events, LinkedIn URL, header rethink. All straightforward CSS/HTML. The parallel events problem (#1, #3) is the most structural -- it needs a layout approach for overlapping timelines.

tier 2 -- what makes it land

Palette discipline. One accent color. The blue is strong enough alone. Use brightness variants for other employers, not entirely different hues.

De-AI the visual language. Remove the grid overlay, replace neon glows with subtle borders, vary card proportions by importance, use proper SVG icons.

Mobile-first hero. Shrink to ~50vh or restructure so the timeline is partially visible on first load. The "Resume PDF" button should be immediately reachable.

OG meta tags. Five lines of HTML that transform how the link looks when shared. Lowest effort, highest impact.

Design directions

Five specialist agents researched this independently and returned concrete recommendations with real-world references. What follows is the synthesized brief.

1. Aesthetic direction

The root cause of the AI look is undifferentiated abundance -- five colors, glows everywhere, identical cards, a billboard hero. The fix is subtraction and hierarchy.

Palette. Go near-monochrome. Don't color-code per employer -- that's what causes the rainbow. Single warm accent for "current role" + links only. If employer differentiation is needed, use desaturated tints at matched lightness applied to a 2px left edge marker.

#0D0F12 bg #16191F surface #E0A458 accent #E8EAED text #9BA1AC muted
Linear (linear.app) -- near-monochrome dark UI with a single accent that appears rarely. Restraint reads as confidence. When color is scarce, the one accent becomes meaningful.

Typography. Pair a grotesque sans with monospace for dates, tech stack, and metadata. Mono on metadata reads as terminal-precise -- the single highest-leverage "engineer cares" signal.

Vercel / Geist (vercel.com) -- Geist Sans + Geist Mono was designed by engineers for engineering interfaces. Also Klim Type Foundry (klim.co.nz) for editorial sans+mono pairing at the highest craft tier.

Timeline. Kill every box-shadow glow. 1px hairline rule for the line. 8--10px dots: hollow ring for past roles, solid accent fill for current role only. Short 16px tick connectors.

Stripe Press / Stripe changelog (stripe.com) -- flat hairline rules with tiny precise markers. Also Rauno Freiberg (rauno.me) for how minimal markers + precise spacing feel intentional.

Hero. Kill the billboard. Replace with a compact, left-aligned masthead: name, title, progression. Timeline starts immediately, above the fold. ~25--30vh, not 92vh.

Paco Coursey (paco.me) and Rauno (rauno.me) -- tiny header, content immediately. Skipping the billboard is the confidence move.

2. Parallel events layout

Recommended: marginalia / sidenote spine. Work history is the main spine. Education lives in a left margin gutter, smaller and dimmer, vertically aligned to the work era it overlaps. The spine is a CSS grid column (not absolute positioned), so it can never drift.

css grid structure
grid-template-columns: [aside] minmax(0,12rem) [spine] 1.25rem [card] minmax(0,1fr)

On mobile, the aside collapses as an indented annotation below its parent card with a dashed left border. No connector math survives to break.

Tufte CSS sidenotes (edwardtufte.github.io/tufte-css) -- margin annotations on desktop, inline on mobile, same mental model.

Alternative: nested "meanwhile" inset. Education becomes a child of the work card it overlaps with. A child literally cannot mis-position relative to its parent. Bulletproof, zero connector logic.

LinkedIn experience entries with nested sub-roles/promotions. read.cv stacked entries.

Key fix: replace left:150px absolute spine with a named grid column. The rail lives inside the layout flow and re-aligns at any width.

3. Card and icon redesign

Core insight: kill the 112px icon column. Move the icon onto the timeline spine as a 44px squircle node. This single move fixes the identical-card problem, the oversized-icon problem, and the mobile reflow problem simultaneously.

hierarchy
Current role (Microsoft): filled node + tinted card surface, expanded content
Prior roles (Apple, Navy): outline nodes, standard surface, compressed content
Education / schools: no card chrome, bare text with small gray node
Brian Lovin (brianlovin.com), Paco Coursey (paco.me), Read.cv -- they vary density per entry. Varied weight reads as a person making hierarchy decisions. Also Tailwind UI Spotlight resume component -- the closest structural match.

Icons: replace Unicode with inline SVG role-meaning marks. Anchor for Navy, signal waves for Apple RF, four-pane window for Microsoft. These communicate the work, not the logo.

Skill tags: replace the generic pill-with-border with tinted flat chips in monospace. No border, org-accent tint, tiny leading dot. Monospace tags (RF, SATCOM, WDK) read as technical tokens.

Vercel / Geist design system -- flat tinted tokens instead of bordered pills. GitHub profile activity timeline -- node-on-spine with clear primary/secondary distinction.

4. Mobile strategy

Hero: drop to min-height: 60svh on mobile. Let the first timeline card peek above the fold to signal "scroll = substance."

Brittany Chiang (brittanychiang.com) -- name, role, and positioning statement immediately visible on mobile. No full-screen splash.

Navigation: don't hide it -- relocate. A persistent bottom action bar with Resume PDF (primary) and Path (secondary). The Resume link is literally what a recruiter came for.

Stripe (stripe.com) on mobile -- keeps primary CTA always reachable. read.cv career profiles -- high-value actions in a slim persistent bar.

Touch targets: every interactive element gets minimum 48x48px hit area.

GOV.UK Design System -- the reference implementation for accessible mobile touch targets.

Open Graph tags: generate a 1200x630 OG image card with name and role baked in as text.

Lee Robinson (leerob.io) -- dynamic OG images so every shared link previews as a branded card.

5. Voice and copy

The core insight: Matt's three jobs aren't three different careers -- they're the same job at increasing scale. He has always worked at the seam between hardware and software. Navy = signals, can't fail. Apple = the physical-to-digital boundary, shipped to millions. Microsoft drivers = literally the software layer that talks to hardware.

recommended hero copy
eyebrow Systems engineer -- hardware to platform
headline I keep the seam between hardware and software reliable.
lede Three employers, one constant -- the layer where physical hardware becomes dependable software. Navy electronics taught me signals, Apple taught me scale, Microsoft taught me the platform underneath.
card headlines as a triptych
navy Learned to fix what couldn't fail.
apple Scaled that to millions of devices.
microsoft Now I build the tooling that keeps it reliable.

Read in sequence, they tell a three-act story. "That" and "Now" stitch the cards together.

contact cta
Have a hard systems problem? I'd like to hear it.
Stripe -- value-prop headline states impact, not skills. Linear -- confidence through brevity. Brian Lovin (brianlovin.com) -- clear first-person positioning. Josh Comeau (joshwcomeau.com) -- warm personal voice without losing technical credibility.

A skill list is the weakest possible headline because every engineer has one. A headline should be the one thing only this person would write.
