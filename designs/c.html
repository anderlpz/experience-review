Phase 01  /  2008–2013  /  U.S. Navy

I learned to find the fault before it found us.

Five years as an IT and Communications Specialist in the United States Navy. My job was keeping satellite communications live on vessels where a dead link wasn't an inconvenience — it was a mission failure.

I worked across the full RF stack: SHF and EHF systems, SATCOM terminals, signal routing, and cryptographic equipment. When you work long enough in systems where you don't get to restart and try again, fault isolation stops being a skill and starts being a reflex.

Every troubleshooting methodology I've used since traces back to the discipline built here.

Phase 02  /  2013–2022  /  Apple

This work shipped in hundreds of millions of iPhones and AirPods.

Nine years at Apple in acoustic and audio systems engineering. The scale of Apple's hardware program is something you can't fully appreciate until you're inside it — tolerance requirements measured in fractions of a decibel, signal chain discipline from chip to enclosure, relentless iteration from prototype to mass production.

My work focused on audio system validation and acoustic measurement: getting the physics right so that when a new device shipped, performance matched specification at scale — across millions of units, in the hands of real people, in real environments.

Every iPhone and AirPods Pro generation I worked on started as a physics problem before it became a product.

Phase 03  /  2022–2025  /  Microsoft

Now I build the tooling every Windows driver has to clear before it ships.

Systems engineer on the Windows Driver Kit and hardware compatibility toolchain. Driver Verifier, the Windows HLK, and the WDK are the gatekeepers for driver quality across the Windows ecosystem — every hardware partner, every OEM, clears their drivers through this stack before they ship.

My role sits at the intersection of driver architecture, test infrastructure, and the engineering systems that enforce correctness at scale. A subtle bug in this tooling doesn't affect one product — it cascades to thousands of driver teams downstream. Precision is not optional.

The same instinct I built in the Navy — find the fault before it finds you — turns out to be exactly the right disposition for this work.
