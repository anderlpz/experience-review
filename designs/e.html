The Through-Line

Same seam, every scale

It's the same job at increasing scale. The Navy taught signals — how electromagnetic energy moves through systems and what happens when something gets in the way. Shipboard SATCOM terminals, radar systems, military RF equipment. Hardware where fault isolation is a discipline, not a convenience.

Apple was the same problem at consumer scale. Make the radio behave inside a device that fits in your hand, then prove it works before the production line starts moving. Hundreds of millions of units. Same physics, different stakes.

“Probably the only resume with both AirPods and Driver Verifier on it.”

Microsoft moved the focus from the physical boundary to the logical one. Driver Verifier, WDK, HLK — tooling that sits between hardware and the operating system. Every Windows driver passes through these gates. The failure gets caught in the lab, not in someone's living room.

Three employers. Three scales. Always at the same seam — the boundary where hardware meets software, where the physical world becomes digital, and where things break if nobody's paying attention.

That's different from your average software engineer. Most people choose a lane — pure software, pure hardware, embedded, cloud. This career is a progression across the full spectrum of technology, held together by one instinct: find the seam, make sure it holds.
